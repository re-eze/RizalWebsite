Summary

Summary

Jose Protacio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda was born on June 19, 1861 and 7th of the eleven children of Don Francisco Mercado and Dona Teodora Alonso. Rizal’s paternal great-great grandfather, Chinese merchant, Domingo Lam-co, adopted the name “Mercado”, which means “market”. Jose’s father became primarily a farmer, he then adopted the surname “Rizal” (Ricial meaning “the green of young growth” or “green fields”). Rizal was born in Calamba, Laguna. The first massive stone house in Calamba was the birthplace of our national hero.

-Rizal’s first memory was his happy days in their family garden when he was three years old. He was weak, sickly, and undersized, he was then given the fondest care by his parents, so his father built a nipa cottage for Pepe to play in daytime.

-At the age of five, he learned to read the Spanish family Bible.

-As a child, he loved to go to the chapel, pray, participate in novenas, and join religious processions.

-Also at the age of five, Rizal began to make pencil sketches and mold in clay and wax objects.

-At age six, his sisters laughed at him for spending much time making clay and wax images to which he prophetically responded, “Alright laugh at me now! Someday when I die, people will make monuments and images of me’.

-When Jose was seven, he experienced riding a casco with his father on their way to a pilgrimage in Antipolo.

-Jose received a pony named “Alipato” from his father. He loved to ride the pony or take a long walk with his black dog named “Usman".

-Before he was eight, he had written a drama which was performed at a local festival; the manuscript of the drama was purchased for two pesos by a gobernadorcillo from Paete.

-Contrary to common knowledge, Rizal did not write the Filipino poem “Sa Aking mga Kababata/Kabata”

-The young Rizal was also interested in magic. He learned different tricks – making a coin disappear & making a handkerchief vanish in thin air.

Rizal' Journey

Rizal on Abroad

Jose Rizal, born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, Philippines, hailed from a prosperous family with educated parents, Francisco Mercado and Teodora Alonzo Realonda. His educational journey included studies at Ateneo Municipal de Manila and the University of Santo Tomas in Manila before pursuing medicine, literature, and political science in Spain. Rizal became a prolific writer, penning influential works such as "Noli Me Tangere" and "El Filibusterismo," which exposed the injustices of Spanish colonial rule and fueled nationalist sentiments.

A proponent of peaceful reform, Rizal advocated for change through education and enlightenment rather than armed revolution. Despite facing persecution, he continued his intellectual pursuits during periods of exile, including in Dapitan in Mindanao. Rizal's association with the Propaganda Movement, a group of Filipino intellectuals seeking political reforms, marked a significant chapter in his activism.

Spain: Rizal's first significant journey abroad was to Spain, where he pursued higher education. He attended the Universidad Central de Madrid, studying medicine, literature, and political science. During his stay in Spain, he actively participated in the Propaganda Movement, advocating for reforms in the Philippines.

Germany: Rizal continued his studies in Heidelberg, Germany, where he conducted research and completed his second novel, "El Filibusterismo." His exposure to European thought and culture influenced his perspective on governance and social issues.

France: Rizal spent some time in France, particularly in Paris, where he continued to refine his ideas and engage with other intellectuals. He frequented libraries and museums, absorbing knowledge and inspiration for his literary and political works.

Belgium: Rizal visited Brussels, Belgium, where he continued his writing and political activities. It was during this time that he contributed articles to Filipino publications and continued his efforts to expose the injustices of Spanish rule in the Philippines.

Austria: Rizal briefly visited Austria, where he continued his studies and observations of European society. He maintained correspondence with fellow Filipino expatriates and continued his advocacy for reforms.

Switzerland: Rizal spent some time in Switzerland, notably in Wilhelmsfeld, where he worked on his annotations to Antonio Morga's "Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas." This work aimed to provide historical context and counter Spanish colonial propaganda about the Philippines.

United States: Rizal traveled to the United States, particularly to San Francisco and other cities on the west coast. He observed American society and governance, comparing it to the situation in the Philippines. His experiences in the U.S. contributed to his understanding of governance and nationalism.

Japan: Rizal visited Japan during his return journey to the Philippines. He admired the discipline and progress of the Japanese society, finding inspiration for potential reforms in his homeland.

Acquaintances

People Rizal Met During His Travel Abroad


In Spain

Paterno Brothers (Antonio, Pedro, and Maximo) - Friends of Rizal shared his ideas; they often held meetings and celebrations in their home.

Don Pablo Ortega y Rey - A rich mestizo who was a former mayor of Manila; befriended Rizal due to him being in love with her daughter.

Consuelo Ortega y Rey - Rizal met her in Madrid while he was studying. A young and beautiful woman, further described in Rizal’s poem dedicated to her.

Senor Roses - the owner of the book store where Rizal always buy

Marcelo H. Del Pilar - An Illustrado and a leading figure of the propaganda movement. Founder of the famous La Solidaridad and a respected member of the freemasons.

Mariano Ponce - Another principal member of the Propaganda Movement. Also, the co-founder of La Solidaridad, together with Marcelo del Pilar.



In France

Maximo Viola - A very good friend of Rizal. The savior of Noli Me Tangere because he helped Rizal when he became near destitute.

Dr. Louis de Weckert - A leading French ophthalmologist. Rizal worked for him as an assistant.

Paz Pardo de Tavera - A pretty girl and fiancée of his good friend, Juan Luna

Nellie Boustead - A beautiful Parisian. The very girl who became the reason for the momentary rivalry between Rizal and Antonio Luna, the famous Revolutionary General.



In Germany

Dr. Otto Becker - Another well-known ophthalmologist. Rizal worked for him as an assistant.

Dr. Karl Ullmer - Rizal stayed at his home. He welcomed Rizal with open arms proving he was a kind protestor.

Prof. Frederich Ratzel - A famous German historian. Rizal befriended him.

Hans Meyer - A German anthropologist. He is a friend of Rizal.

Dr. Adolf B. Meyer - He is the Director of the Anthropological Museum. Also, befriended by Rizal.

Dr. Feodor Jagor - A celebrated scientist-traveler. Author of the book: Travels in the Philippines, which Rizal read as a child.

Dr. Hans Virchow - Introduced to Rizal by Dr. Jagor. He is a famous German anthropologist.

Dr. Ernest Schwelgger - A German ophthalmologist. Rizal worked for him too.



In Bohemia

Prof. Ferdinand Blumentritt - A Kind-hearted Austrian professor; developed a lifelong friendship with Rizal.

Burgo Master - A kind town Mayor who welcomed Rizal.

Dr. Carlos Czepelak - A renowned scientist in Europe; he met Rizal at Leitmeritz.

Prof. Robert Klutschak - An eminent naturist. Also met Rizal in Leitmeritz.

Dr. Willkomm - He's working as a professor of natural history at the University of Prague.



In Hongkong

Jose Maria Basa - A Filipino resident in Hong Kong; he toured Rizal in Macau.

Jose Slanz de Varanda - A Spaniard who served as a secretary of Gov. Gen. Terrero and became a spy by shadowing Rizal’s movements in Hong Kong.



In Macau

Don Juan Francisco Lecaros - A Filipino gentleman, who accommodated Rizal and Basa in his home.



In London

Dr. Antonio Ma. Regidor - A practicing lawyer where Rizal stayed at his home as a guest.

Dr. Reinhold Rost - A librarian of the Ministry of Foreign Affairs. The one who recommended Rizal to the British Museum authorities and famously called him "a pearl of a man."



In Japan

Seiko Usui (O-Sei-San) - Lover of Jose Rizal in Japan. She taught him the Japanese language, which he mastered within a week—the reason why Rizal almost got to settle in Japan.

Tetcho Suehiro - A fighting Japanese journalist who was forced into exile by the government; met Rizal on the Belgic and became friends.

Juan Perez Caballero - The secretary of the Spanish Legation in Hong Kong and invited Rizal to reside in the Spanish Legation.
